# Fine Hair

Use when:
- `thickness = fine`
- or the user says their hair is fine, easily weighed down, flat, limp, low-volume, greasy quickly, or hard to keep bouncy

Advisor interpretation:
- Fine hair is strand diameter, not automatically low density, damage, or low strength.
- Weight, residue, and product placement matter more than "extra rich" repair language.
- Prefer lightweight support and good distribution over stacking many rich steps.
- Fine hair can still be dry, curly, damaged, or color-treated; keep support targeted rather than absent.

Category implications:
- Shampoo: Favor scalp-appropriate cleansing and residue awareness; avoid making the roots heavier.
- Conditioner: Use lightweight to medium support mainly on lengths and ends, with cautious dosage near roots.
- Leave-in: Start small, distribute well, and prefer light formulas or dual-role products when a leave-in is useful.
- Mask: Keep optional and brief; prefer light or medium weight unless selected product data supports stronger intensity.
- Oil: Use high caution; if relevant, tiny end-only amounts are safer than scalp or mid-length oiling.
- Bondbuilder / repair: Relevant only when damage signals exist; do not frame fine hair itself as needing repair.

Routine implications:
- Keep the core routine lean, then add only the one step that solves a visible issue such as tangles, heat protection, or dry ends.
- If hair feels flat, heavy, or coated, reduce layers before adding more care.

Avoid:
- Do not equate fine hair with thin hair, hair loss, or fragility.
- Do not stack rich conditioner, mask, leave-in, and oil unless the profile gives a clear reason.
- Do not make volume loss sound inevitable or unsolvable.
- Do not ignore curl, damage, or dryness just because hair is fine.

Ask only if:
- density, length dryness, heat use, or curl pattern would materially change amount, placement, or product weight.

Proactive next step:
- Offer a lightweight amount-and-placement rule or a minimal routine that preserves softness without flattening.
