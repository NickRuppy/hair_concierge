# Peeling: Core Fit

## Runtime Variables

- `profile.scalp_type`
- `profile.scalp_condition`
- `profile.concerns`
- `profile.thickness`
- product-residue, oily-root, flake, itch, and sensitivity signals

## Category Role

Peeling is an occasional scalp-exfoliation or scalp-scrub category for cosmetic buildup, oil, and mild flake support on a tolerant scalp. It is not a default wash step and not a treatment for inflamed, painful, persistent, or diagnosed scalp conditions.

## Best Fit

- oily scalp with visible residue, product film, or mild cosmetic buildup
- mild non-painful flakes where the user is asking about a cosmetic scalp reset, not medical treatment
- users who tolerate scalp products well and want an occasional add-on
- routines with heavy styling products, dry shampoo, oils, or co-washing that may leave residue

## Weak Fit

- irritated, burning, painful, wounded, inflamed, or very sensitive scalp
- persistent flakes, intense itch, redness, suspected dermatitis, psoriasis, infection, or hair-loss contexts
- dry or fragile hair when the proposed method is harsh mechanical scrubbing
- daily use or aggressive exfoliation as routine maintenance

## Decision Axes

- `buildup_vs_symptom`: cosmetic residue supports peeling; persistent itch, pain, redness, or inflammation does not
- `method_tolerance`: sensitive or dry scalps need gentler, less mechanical approaches or no peeling
- `frequency_tolerance`: occasional and response-based beats fixed aggressive cadence
- `routine_residue`: dry shampoo, oils, stylers, co-wash, or heavy masks can strengthen a mild reset case

## Profile Interplay

Oily or buildup-prone scalps can make occasional peeling more plausible, especially when the current routine uses residue-prone products. Sensitive, dry, dandruff-prone, irritated, chemically stressed, or fragile profiles should be handled conservatively and may be better served by gentler cleansing or medical evaluation when symptoms persist.

## Compare Against Other Categories

- Compare against shampoo when the user needs normal scalp cleansing.
- Compare against deep cleansing shampoo when product film or mineral buildup needs a cleanser-based reset rather than scrub friction.
- Compare against dry shampoo when the user wants temporary oil absorption rather than residue removal.
- Compare against medical scalp guidance when symptoms are persistent, painful, inflamed, or disease-like.

## Answer Guidance

- Frame peeling as occasional, optional scalp support for buildup-prone but tolerant scalps.
- Ask or infer whether this is cosmetic residue or a symptom pattern before recommending it.
- Emphasize gentle pressure, scalp-only placement, and stopping if burning, soreness, or irritation appears.
- Keep frequency conservative and tolerance-based rather than universal.
- Do not name products unless the active tool path has selected products.

## Guardrails

- Do not recommend strong mechanical or chemical exfoliation on irritated, inflamed, painful, wounded, or very sensitive scalps.
- Do not treat persistent flakes, dandruff, hair loss, or scalp disease as cosmetic peeling problems.
- Do not imply that more frequent exfoliation is better.

## Source Note

AAD safe-exfoliation guidance says method and frequency should match skin tolerance and warns that over-exfoliation can cause redness and irritation. Mayo Clinic dandruff guidance supports conservative escalation when flakes or itch persist despite appropriate care.
