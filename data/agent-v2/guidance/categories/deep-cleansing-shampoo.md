# Deep Cleansing Shampoo

## Role In Hair Concierge
Deep-cleansing shampoo is an occasional reset lane for hair or scalp that feels coated, waxy, limp, greasy, unresponsive, mineral-loaded, or overloaded by product layers.

It is not the everyday shampoo baseline, not structural repair, not hair-loss support, not a scalp-disease treatment, and not a cure for color fade that already happened.

Shampoo application can focus on scalp/roots, but deep-cleansing shampoo is not conceptually scalp-only. It may be the better reset when product/mineral film through lengths or hard-water feel points toward a reset/clarifying/chelating shampoo rather than a scalp exfoliation product.

## Use When
- The user asks for Tiefenreinigung, clarifying, chelating, detox, buildup reset, or a reset wash.
- Hair feels coated, waxy, sticky, heavy, greasy-feeling, limp, dull, stiff, brassy, or unresponsive after normal washing.
- The routine includes heavy stylers, dry shampoo, oiling, co-washing, CWC/OWC film, rich masks, leave-ins, or many leave-on layers.
- Hard water, pool exposure, metal/mineral concern, or oxidative color/bleach service prep is part of the question.
- The scalp has mild cosmetic oil, residue, or visible buildup without pain, inflammation, wounds, unusual shedding, or persistent symptom language.
- Product/mineral film through lengths, coated lengths, or hard-water feel suggests a cleanser-based reset lane rather than scalp peeling.

## Best Fit
- product film or residue that normal shampoo is not resolving
- hard-water, pool, calcium, iron, copper, metal, dull/stiff/brassy, or service-prep signals
- product/mineral film through lengths or hard-water feel where reset/clarifying/chelating shampoo is more plausible than scalp-only exfoliation
- mild non-medical scalp/root residue where a wash-based reset is more appropriate than adding care layers
- buildup that may be mimicking dryness, frizz, flatness, rough feel, or lack of definition
- routines that need simplify-reset-rebuild logic before richer conditioner, leave-in, mask, or oil escalation

## Weak Fit / Not The Best Lever
- ordinary daily cleansing, sweat, or light oil where normal shampoo is enough
- dry/rough lengths, shine, softness, frizz, or split ends without coating or residue signals
- fragile, very dry, fine, curly, color-treated, chemically stressed, or sensitive profiles when the only proposed move is a harsh full-length reset
- itchy, burning, inflamed, painful, wounded, pustular, persistently flaky, unusual shedding, patchy loss, diagnosed, or disease-like scalp contexts
- persistent itch, redness, burning, pain, repeated flakes, or shedding belongs to the safety boundary, not stronger peeling or stronger reset advice
- structural repair, regrowth, permanent smoothing, or already-happened color fade

## Realistic Benefit
A proportional reset can make hair feel cleaner, lighter, less coated, and more responsive to styling or conditioning. It can also clarify whether the issue was under-care or over-layering.

The reset job matters. A clarifying reset addresses product film and residue; a chelating reset is the better lane for hard-water, pool, or metal deposits; scalp-exfoliation language belongs to scalp buildup and may overlap with the peeling category. Deep cleansing does not repair damaged hair, regrow hair, treat scalp disease, or replace normal shampoo on every wash.

## Category Boundaries
Belongs here:
- clarifying shampoo for product film, stylers, silicone-like residue, dry-shampoo residue, oils, masks, leave-ins, co-washing, CWC/OWC film, or coated feel
- chelating shampoo when hard-water, pool, calcium, iron, copper, metal, dull/stiff/brassy feel, or pre-service metal reset is plausible
- wash-based scalp/root reset for mild cosmetic oil or residue
- reset-and-rebuild routine explanations when buildup is plausible

Does not belong here:
- normal shampoo for the everyday scalp/root wash baseline
- dry shampoo as a temporary root freshness bridge
- peeling as a separate scalp exfoliation product category
- conditioner, mask, leave-in, oil, or bondbuilder care
- medical scalp symptoms, hair loss, or inflammatory scalp treatment

Common confusions:
- A clarifier removes product film and residue; do not call every clarifier a chelator.
- A chelator targets hard-water, pool, or metal deposits such as calcium, iron, or copper; do not use chelation language for plain product buildup.
- Scalp exfoliation works on scalp skin/sebum residue, not fibre mineral deposits.
- Product/mineral film through lengths or hard-water feel points toward reset/clarifying/chelating shampoo; scalp-local residue on tolerant skin may point toward gentle occasional scalp peeling.
- Persistent itch, redness, burning, pain, repeated flakes, or shedding is a safety boundary, not stronger peeling.
- Hair oiling can soften or loosen sebum as a pre-wash idea, but it is not a clarifier or chelator.
- A generic `detox` label does not prove one product does all reset jobs.

## Reset Lane Logic
Name the likely reset job before recommending action:

- `Rückstands-Reset`: product film, stylers, masks, leave-ins, oils, co-washing, CWC/OWC, dry shampoo, coated feel, sticky feel, limpness, or "nothing gets clean".
- `Kalk-/Metall-Reset`: hard water, pool, metal/mineral concern, dull/stiff/brassy feel, or oxidative color/bleach service prep.
- `Kopfhaut-Reset`: mild non-medical scalp oil, visible residue, or scalp-side buildup.

`Kopfhaut-Reset` is an internal lane label. In customer-facing German, prefer softer wording such as `Ansatz-/Kopfhaut-Rückstands-Reset`, `Rückstände am Ansatz`, or `Ansatz ist belegt`, so the answer does not sound like medical scalp treatment.

If the signal is only ordinary oiliness, sweat, or light residue, compare normal shampoo before escalating to deep cleansing. If the signal is mainly scalp-local residue or oily-root buildup on tolerant skin, compare gentle occasional scalp peeling. If the signal is product/mineral film through lengths or hard-water feel, compare reset/clarifying/chelating shampoo. If the signal is dry lengths without coating, route toward conditioner, leave-in, mask, oil, bondbuilder, or technique rather than reset-first advice.

For dry, fine, curly, bleached, color-treated, fragile, or sensitive profiles, keep reset language gentler, less frequent, and explicitly need-based. Follow reset washes with conditioner or suitable length care, but do not invent exact product order or timing.

## Agent May Decide
- Whether the reset case is strong, optional, weak, or unsafe.
- Whether the likely lane is product film, minerals/pool exposure, or mild scalp buildup.
- Whether normal shampoo, peeling, conditioner, leave-in, mask, oil, bondbuilder, troubleshooting, or safety guidance is more appropriate.
- Whether one follow-up would materially change the reset lane, product safety, or safety posture.
- How to explain reset logic in customer-facing German.

## Code And Tools Decide
- Concrete product IDs, category membership, recommendation order, lifecycle, availability, price, stock, retailer, and product count.
- Whether a product is clarifying, chelating, scalp-exfoliating, color-safe, service-prep suitable, gentle, compatible, or supported for a claimed reset lane.
- Exact cadence, exact timing, exact placement, exact protocol, compatibility, and product-specific claims.
- Saved routine state and routine mutations.

## Required Grounding
Use `select_products` before naming concrete deep-cleansing products as recommendations.

Use product metadata before claiming chelation, hard-water or metal removal, color safety, pre-service support, scalp-exfoliation effects, compatibility, exact cadence, or exact protocol.

For named-product chelating, hard-water, metal-removal, or color-safe questions, do not answer from the product name, `detox` wording, or marketing family. If selected metadata does not support the claim, say it cannot be safely promised for that variant, explain which reset facts are grounded, and avoid asking for photos or links unless the current tools can ingest and ground those inputs.

Use routine tooling before creating or changing a saved routine.

## Missing Required Data
Ask at most one follow-up if missing information would materially change:
- product buildup versus hard water/pool exposure versus scalp residue
- whether the concern is ordinary oiliness, fibre coating, or scalp symptoms
- whether very dry, porous, fragile, curly, color-treated, chemically stressed, or damaged hair changes tolerance
- whether an oxidative color or bleach service is coming up
- whether a named-product detail question lacks the exact product
- whether a saved/current routine mutation needs current routine context

Do not ask a follow-up if a safe, useful general reset answer is possible.

## Safety Boundary
Escalate away from cosmetic reset advice when the user reports scalp pain, burning, significant irritation, wounds, swelling, pustules, unusual shedding, patchy hair loss, infection-like symptoms, or persistent inflammatory symptoms.

If symptoms are linked to a reset product, suggest pausing the suspected trigger and getting professional evaluation when symptoms are significant, persistent, painful, patchy, or inflammatory. Do not diagnose, and do not intensify cleansing or exfoliation as the first move.

## German Answer Shape
Start with the practical lane: `Rückstands-Reset`, `Kalk-/Metall-Reset`, `Ansatz-/Kopfhaut-Rückstands-Reset`, or "normal shampoo is enough".

Then explain:
1. why the reset fits or does not fit the user's signal
2. that it is occasional and need-based, not the default every-wash shampoo
3. that a reset wash temporarily replaces the normal shampoo step, then length care follows
4. whether product choice, exact cadence, compatibility, or service-prep claims need product data

Use everyday German terms like `Rückstände`, `Ansatz`, `Längen`, `belegt`, `wachsiges Gefühl`, `Kalk/Metalle`, `normale Wäsche`, and `sanft wieder aufbauen`.

## Do Not
- Do not make deep cleansing a daily shampoo default; instead frame it as occasional and need-based.
- Do not collapse clarifying, chelating, and scalp exfoliation into one generic detox recommendation; instead name the likely reset job.
- Do not mention hard water, metal removal, color safety, or chelating from buildup signals alone; instead ask or explain that those claims need product/context data.
- Do not present deep cleansing as structural repair, hair-loss support, scalp-disease treatment, or a fix for already-happened color fade; instead route to care, safety, or expectations as appropriate.
- Do not recommend aggressive DIY salt/sugar scrubs or claim deep cleansing `opens the cuticle` as the mechanism; instead keep the mechanism to residue/mineral removal when grounded.
- Do not name products, rank products, or make product-specific claims unless tool/catalog data grounded them; instead answer at category level or use `select_products`.
