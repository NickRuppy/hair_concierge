# Scalp And Medical-Adjacent Boundaries

External evidence note for AgentV2 goal/concern lever guidance.

## Scoped Coverage

- Goal: `healthy_scalp`
- Concerns: `hair_loss`, `dandruff`, `oily_scalp`, `thinning`
- Scope boundary: cosmetic scalp hygiene, mild oiliness, mild flakes, scalp comfort, and product/routine irritation signals.
- Out of scope for product-shopping answers: diagnosis, medical treatment, hair regrowth claims, sudden shedding, patchy loss, persistent dandruff, scalp pain, burning, redness, swelling, sores, pus, scarring, or inflammatory scalp disease.

## Source List

- [American Academy of Dermatology - Tips for healthy hair](https://www.aad.org/public/everyday-care/hair-scalp-care/hair/healthy-hair-tips): wash based on oil/dirt, shampoo the scalp, condition lengths, and adjust cadence by oily scalp vs dry/curly/thick hair.
- [American Academy of Dermatology - How to treat dandruff](https://www.aad.org/public/everyday-care/hair-scalp-care/scalp/treat-dandruff): most dandruff does not need medical care, but persistent or severe dandruff should be evaluated by a board-certified dermatologist.
- [American Academy of Dermatology - Seborrheic dermatitis treatment](https://www.aad.org/public/diseases/a-z/seborrheic-dermatitis-treatment): dandruff shampoos can help mild to moderate scalp seborrheic dermatitis; cadence depends on hair pattern; irritation or no improvement warrants dermatologist input.
- [Cochrane - Antifungal treatments for seborrhoeic dermatitis](https://www.cochrane.org/evidence/CD008138_antifungal-treatments-applied-skin-treat-seborrhoeic-dermatitis): ketoconazole and ciclopirox show benefit vs placebo for seborrhoeic dermatitis, with study variation.
- [Mayo Clinic - Seborrheic dermatitis diagnosis and treatment](https://www.mayoclinic.org/diseases-conditions/seborrheic-dermatitis/diagnosis-treatment/drc-20352714): medicated shampoos are common first treatments; cadence and contact time matter; ketoconazole may worsen dryness in tightly coiled or chemically treated hair.
- [eCFR 21 CFR Part 358 Subpart H](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-21/chapter-I/subchapter-D/part-358/subpart-H): U.S. OTC monograph for dandruff, seborrheic dermatitis, and psoriasis drug products, current on eCFR as of June 24, 2026.
- [American Academy of Dermatology - Hair loss signs and symptoms](https://www.aad.org/public/diseases/hair-loss/insider/begin): burning, stinging, intense itch, tenderness, scaly bald patches, sores, pus, redness, swelling, and psoriasis-like scale can accompany hair loss and point to medical causes.
- [American Academy of Dermatology - Hair shedding](https://www.aad.org/public/diseases/hair-loss/insider/shedding): 50-100 hairs/day can be normal; excessive shedding can follow stressors such as childbirth, illness, fever, surgery, weight loss, or stress.
- [American Academy of Dermatology - Hair loss causes](https://www.aad.org/public/diseases/hair-loss/causes/18-causes): scalp infection, medication, hormonal imbalance, and scalp psoriasis can cause hair loss; stopping medication without a doctor is unsafe.
- [DermNet - Hair loss](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/hair-loss): alopecia may be localized or diffuse and may involve scalp disease or scarring; treatment depends on diagnosis.
- [DermNet - Diffuse alopecia](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/diffuse-alopecia): telogen effluvium causes excessive shedding after triggers; pattern hair loss causes gradual thinning; scalp exam should assess inflammation, redness, scaling, and scarring.
- [NHS - Hair loss](https://www.nhs.uk/symptoms/hair-loss/): see a GP if worried about hair loss and before considering commercial hair clinics; causes include illness, stress, weight loss, and iron deficiency.
- [HSE - Hair loss](https://www2.hse.ie/conditions/hair-loss/): see a GP for sudden hair loss, clumps, itchy or sore hair-loss areas, or worry; no shampoo helps hair regrow.
- [AAD - Dry scalp conditions that may be serious](https://www.aad.org/public/everyday-care/hair-scalp-care/scalp/dry-scalp-conditions): scaly patches plus hair loss or broken hairs can indicate tinea capitis, which needs prescription antifungal treatment.
- [British Association of Dermatologists - Seborrhoeic dermatitis](https://www.bad.org.uk/pils/seborrhoeic-dermatitis): seborrhoeic dermatitis is red, itchy, flaky inflammation in oily zones; dandruff/scalp seborrhoeic dermatitis is common, but inflammatory symptoms are more than a simple cosmetic flake issue.

## Practical Lever Map

### `healthy_scalp`

- `strong`: Match shampoo cadence to scalp oil, sweat, dirt, and buildup. Oily scalp may need more frequent cleansing; dry/curly/coily/thick hair often needs less frequent shampooing and more length protection.
- `strong`: Apply shampoo primarily to the scalp. Treat the scalp as the cleansing target; let rinse-down clean lengths unless there is heavy product buildup.
- `strong`: Use conditioner to protect lengths and reduce dryness/tangling. Fine or straight hair usually needs conditioner mainly on ends; dry or curly hair may need more length coverage.
- `moderate`: For mild scalp comfort concerns without red flags, reduce irritants: avoid harsh scrubbing, very hot water, aggressive exfoliation, heavy occlusive oils on an already oily scalp, or stacking many actives.
- `moderate`: If mild flakes coexist with oiliness, use an anti-dandruff shampoo category according to label directions and hair type. Frame as controlling flakes/itch/irritation, not curing a disease.
- `weak`: Scalp oils, massages, "detoxes", scrubs, and natural remedies as first-line scalp health answers. They may feel nice for some users but can irritate, worsen oiliness, or delay care.

### `oily_scalp`

- `strong`: Increase cleansing frequency when the scalp is visibly oily, itchy from buildup, sweaty, or product-heavy.
- `strong`: Keep shampoo at the scalp and conditioner away from the roots if hair gets flat or greasy quickly.
- `moderate`: Lightweight, non-occlusive styling and fewer root-applied oils/leave-ins may reduce greasy feel.
- `moderate`: If oiliness comes with recurrent flakes/itch, consider anti-dandruff category guidance because dandruff/seborrhoeic dermatitis often occurs in oily scalp zones.
- `unknown`: "Training" the scalp to produce less oil by washing less. Do not present this as evidence-based runtime advice.

### `dandruff`

- `strong`: Mild flakes without pain, redness, swelling, sores, patchy hair loss, or systemic symptoms can start with routine hygiene plus an anti-dandruff shampoo category.
- `strong`: If flakes persist after correct use, worsen, are severe, or include scalp redness/swelling/pain, recommend dermatologist/medical evaluation.
- `moderate`: Anti-dandruff active categories can control flakes, scaling, itch, irritation, and recurrence when used as directed. Evidence is strongest for regulated symptom-control claims, weaker for choosing one active as universally best.
- `moderate`: Contact time matters for medicated shampoos; users should follow label directions or clinician advice.
- `weak`: Rotating actives may help when one stops working, but this should be framed as a dermatologist-guided or cautious next step, not a deterministic rule.
- `do not`: Diagnose seborrheic dermatitis, psoriasis, eczema, fungal infection, or allergy from chat symptoms. Say these can look similar and need a clinician if persistent/severe.

### `hair_loss`

- `strong`: Normal shedding can be 50-100 hairs/day. Excessive shedding, sudden shedding, clumps, patchy bald areas, visible thinning, or loss with scalp symptoms needs medical evaluation.
- `strong`: Hair loss can have medical, hormonal, medication, infection, autoimmune, inflammatory, nutritional, stress, postpartum, illness, genetic, and traction causes. Treatment depends on diagnosis.
- `strong negative`: No shampoo should be positioned as a hair-regrowth solution.
- `moderate`: Cosmetic support can still be offered after the medical boundary: gentle cleansing, avoiding traction/tight styles, reducing heat stress, using conditioner to limit breakage, and styling for visual fullness.
- `do not`: Recommend buying an anti-hair-loss shampoo, growth serum, supplement, scalp oil, or exfoliant as the primary fix.

### `thinning`

- `strong`: Gradual thinning, widening part, crown thinning, receding hairline, or reduced density is diagnosis-dependent and should not be treated as a simple product gap.
- `strong`: If thinning is sudden, patchy, painful, itchy/sore, associated with scalp redness/scale/scarring, or psychologically distressing, suggest GP/dermatologist evaluation.
- `moderate`: Cosmetic levers can improve appearance but not reverse the cause: lightweight cleansing, avoiding heavy root products, volumizing styling, gentle detangling, minimizing heat/traction, and haircut/styling choices.
- `weak`: Thickening shampoos and volumizing products can make hair look fuller temporarily; they are styling aids, not treatment.
- `do not`: Promise prevention, regrowth, follicle stimulation, DHT blocking, or "stopping thinning" unless a future medically reviewed, legally safe product/clinical path exists.

## Profile Modifiers

- Fine + oily + straight/wavy: more frequent scalp cleansing may be appropriate; keep conditioner and leave-ins light and mostly off roots; avoid heavy oils as first move.
- Curly/coily/textured or thick: avoid copying straight/oily cadence. Cleanse when needed, protect lengths from dryness, and be careful with medicated shampoo frequency.
- Chemically treated, bleached, colored, relaxed, or heat-damaged hair: anti-dandruff and antifungal shampoos may dry or roughen already vulnerable lengths; use as directed, focus on scalp contact, condition lengths, and avoid overuse.
- Dry lengths + oily scalp: separate scalp and length strategy. Cleanse scalp enough for comfort; condition/protect mids and ends.
- Sensitive scalp or history of eczema/psoriasis/allergy: keep cosmetic advice minimal and non-irritating; persistent flakes, redness, itch, burning, or soreness should escalate.
- Recent childbirth, high fever, major illness, surgery, rapid weight loss, severe stress, medication changes, thyroid/PCOS/iron-deficiency concerns: treat shedding/thinning as medical-context sensitive; recommend GP/derm instead of product shopping.
- Tight hairstyles, extensions, frequent ponytails/buns, wigs with tension: ask about traction if relevant; advise reducing tension, but do not diagnose traction alopecia.
- Product-heavy routine or dry shampoo use: buildup may justify better cleansing cadence; dry shampoo should not replace washing when scalp is oily/itchy/flaky.

## Conflicts

- Oily scalp vs dry curls/lengths: washing enough for scalp comfort can dry lengths. Runtime answer should split scalp cleansing from length conditioning.
- Dandruff control vs color/chemical treatment: medicated shampoos may be necessary for symptoms but can be drying; avoid aggressive frequency unless label/clinician supports it.
- Volume goals vs scalp comfort: heavy oils, rich masks at roots, or leave-ins can flatten fine/oily hair and may worsen greasy feel.
- Hair-loss anxiety vs cosmetic support: giving product tips too early can imply the agent thinks a cosmetic routine can solve a medical concern.
- Mild flakes vs inflammatory disease: dandruff, seborrhoeic dermatitis, psoriasis, eczema, fungal infection, and contact dermatitis can overlap visually; persistent/severe symptoms need diagnosis.

## Runtime Implications

- Start with a boundary split:
  - "mild oil/flakes/comfort without alarming symptoms" -> cosmetic hygiene levers are OK.
  - "hair loss/thinning/sudden shedding/pain/burning/redness/swelling/sores/pus/patchy loss/persistent dandruff" -> recommend medical evaluation first.
- For `healthy_scalp` and `oily_scalp`, prioritize cadence, scalp-targeted cleansing, product placement, and avoiding root heaviness before adding new products.
- For `dandruff`, allow anti-dandruff shampoo category guidance only as symptom control. Include "follow the label" and "see a dermatologist if it does not improve or is severe."
- For `hair_loss` and `thinning`, answer with empathy and a medical boundary first. Then, only if helpful, offer non-treatment cosmetic support such as gentle handling and visual fullness.
- Avoid deterministic diagnosis language:
  - Use: "Das kann verschiedene Ursachen haben" / "Das sollte dermatologisch abgeklärt werden."
  - Avoid: "Das ist nur Kopfhautbelag" / "Du brauchst ein Anti-Haarausfall-Shampoo" / "Dieses Produkt stoppt Haarausfall."
- Avoid product-shopping defaults for medical-adjacent terms:
  - Do not call `select_products` just because the user says hair loss, thinning, sudden shedding, scalp pain, inflammation, or persistent dandruff.
  - If products are discussed, they should be framed as supportive scalp hygiene or appearance support, not treatment.
- Preserve uncertainty in German answer shape:
  - Short concern validation.
  - Boundary sentence when needed.
  - Cosmetic levers only if safe.
  - Minimal missing-data question only if it changes the boundary: duration, severity, sudden vs gradual, patchy vs diffuse, pain/burning/redness, recent illness/postpartum/medication/stress.

## Open Risks

- Regulatory geography: eCFR is U.S.-specific. EU/German OTC/cosmetic classification may differ; runtime product claims should stay generic unless catalog metadata and local compliance are verified.
- Active ingredient availability: some actives vary by market and product category; do not hard-code actives as available in Chaarlie catalog without product data.
- Dandruff vs seborrhoeic dermatitis vs psoriasis/eczema/fungal infection cannot be reliably separated from chat text alone.
- Hair loss can be emotionally high-stakes. Even when likely temporary, the agent should not dismiss worry or delay evaluation.
- "Natural" scalp remedies have weak and uneven evidence and higher irritation risk than many users expect; avoid promoting them as safer by default.
- The line between cosmetic scalp comfort and medical symptoms should remain conservative, especially for pain, burning, inflammation, persistent itch, sores, pus, sudden shedding, and patchy loss.
