# Fiber Surface And Cosmetic Feel

## Scoped Goals And Concerns Covered

Goals: `shine`, `less_frizz`, `moisture`

Concerns: `dryness`, `frizz`, `tangling`

Scope boundary: cosmetic hair fiber feel and appearance only. This note does not cover scalp disease, hair loss, dandruff treatment, or internal nutrition causes except as open-risk boundaries.

## Practical Lever Map

### Shine / `shine`

Most supported first levers:

1. Smooth the surface: rinse-out conditioner, appropriate leave-in, silicone/cationic/polymer film formers, or light oil/serum where appropriate. Evidence: `moderate`.
2. Reduce roughness and friction: gentler shampooing, conditioner after washing, less towel rubbing, less aggressive brushing, lower heat. Evidence: `strong`.
3. Remove dulling residue when signals fit: clarify only when hair feels coated, heavy, waxy, product-laden, or scalp/oil buildup is likely. Evidence: `moderate`.
4. Styling/finish: blow-dry direction/tension, cool-shot, smoothing brush, anti-humidity finish can increase visible alignment/shine. Evidence: `weak` as a practical styling lever, not fiber repair.

Weak first levers:

- Heavy oiling or masks for everyone; can improve shine by coating but may flatten fine hair.
- "Repairing" shine loss as if existing damaged cuticle can be restored to virgin state; better phrase is smoothing/protecting and reducing further damage.

### Less Frizz / `less_frizz` And Concern `frizz`

First split:

- Humidity-reactive frizz: needs film-forming/anti-humidity styling, cuticle smoothing, damage reduction.
- Dry/rough-feeling frizz: often needs conditioning, leave-in, gentler cleansing, less friction, possibly richer care.
- Mechanical frizz: towel rubbing, dry brushing curls, over-brushing, heat, sleep friction, or aggressive detangling.
- Natural texture pattern: curls/waves may need definition and handling, not "damage repair."
- Product mismatch: too little hold/slip, too much buildup, or too-heavy products causing limp frizz.

Most supported first levers:

1. Conditioner plus leave-in where profile fits; immediate reduction in static/frizz and easier combing. Evidence: `strong`.
2. Reduce mechanical disturbance: avoid rubbing with towel, detangle gently, avoid dry brushing curly/coily hair, use sections. Evidence: `strong`.
3. Match product weight and hold to profile: lightweight leave-in/spray for fine or volume-seeking hair; cream/oil/richer conditioner for thick, curly/coily, porous, or very dry lengths. Evidence: `moderate`.
4. Clarify/reset when frizz coexists with heaviness, coated feel, limp roots, or heavy styling/oil use. Evidence: `moderate`.
5. Anti-humidity styling film/serum for weather-triggered frizz after basic conditioning is in place. Evidence: `weak/moderate`.

Weak first levers:

- More oil as the default answer.
- Protein/bond repair as a generic frizz solution without damage signals.
- Telling curly/coily users to brush smooth when dry; dermatology guidance warns this can worsen breakage/frizz.

### Moisture / `moisture` And Concern `dryness`

Runtime translation:

- Treat user-facing "moisture" as softness, slip, reduced roughness, reduced brittle feel, easier detangling, and less frizz. Do not present hair as a living tissue that can be hydrated like skin.

Most supported first levers:

1. Rinse-out conditioner after shampoo; leave-in when hair is long, curly/coily, heat/chemical-treated, dry/brittle, tangled, frizzy, or fine-but-limp needing a lighter formula. Evidence: `strong`.
2. Gentle cleansing: shampoo scalp, let suds run through lengths, avoid rubbing lengths, avoid over-cleansing if dryness follows wash day. Evidence: `strong`.
3. Richer conditioning/masks only when hair is thick, curly/coily, coarse, high-porosity/damaged, bleached, long, or repeatedly dry after normal conditioner. Evidence: `moderate`.
4. Oil/serum as seal/lubricant, not universal hydration. Coconut oil has specific penetration/protein-loss evidence; many oils mainly coat/lubricate. Evidence: `moderate` for coconut oil, `weak` for generic oils.

Weak first levers:

- Recommending very rich masks/oils to fine, low-density, oily-scalp, volume-seeking, or easily weighed-down profiles before lighter conditioning is tried.
- Saying dryness is always lack of water; the source literature notes humidity and water uptake can also drive bad hair behavior.

### Tangling / `tangling`

Most supported first levers:

1. Slip first: rinse-out conditioner, leave-in/detangler, or a small amount of suitable oil/serum on difficult zones. Evidence: `strong`.
2. Saturation/dampness: detangle with water/conditioner support, especially curly/coily hair; avoid dry brushing curls/coils. Evidence: `strong`.
3. Sectioning and tool technique: fingers or wide-tooth comb; start at ends and work upward; reduce pulling. Evidence: `strong`.
4. Reduce friction: towel blot/wrap rather than rub; protect during sleep; avoid high-friction styles/accessories. Evidence: `moderate`.
5. Damage and split-end prevention: trimming can remove damaged ends that keep catching, but it is a maintenance lever, not the first answer to every tangle. Evidence: `moderate`.

Weak first levers:

- Telling the user to brush more.
- Starting with a repair treatment when the immediate bottleneck is slip/technique.
- Treating all tangling as dryness; damage, length, texture, sectioning, and product residue can be just as relevant.

## Profile Modifiers

- Fine hair / low density / volume goal: prefer light conditioner, spray leave-in, small amounts, mid-lengths to ends, and clarify if coated. Heavy masks/oils can make hair limp or greasy. Evidence: `moderate`.
- Thick, coarse, curly, coily, long, or high-density hair: more conditioner, sectioning, leave-in, and sometimes oil/richer masks are more justified. Evidence: `strong` for curly/coily needing more conditioning and detangling support.
- Wavy/curly/coily pattern: frizz may be natural pattern plus disturbance; prioritize definition, wet/damp styling, leave-in/hold, and no dry brushing. Evidence: `strong`.
- Bleached, colored, permed, relaxed, heat-styled, UV/weathered, or chemically treated hair: higher suspicion for lipid loss, roughness, hydrophilicity, friction, tangling, dullness, and humidity reactivity. Evidence: `strong`.
- Oily scalp or heavy styling use: separate scalp/root oiliness from dry lengths; consider buildup/reset before adding richer products. Evidence: `moderate`.
- Low routine tolerance: one rinse-out conditioner or light leave-in may deliver most immediate feel benefit; avoid multi-step masks/oils unless strongly indicated. Evidence: `moderate`.
- Color protection goal: clarifying or harsh cleansing can improve buildup-related dullness but may be a poor default for color-treated/bleached dryness. Evidence: `moderate`.

## Conflicts

- `shine` vs `volume`: shine often benefits from smoothing films; volume can suffer when the film is too heavy.
- `less_frizz` vs `volume`: frizz control may reduce puffiness/body; answer should distinguish unwanted halo/frizz from desired fullness.
- `moisture` vs oily scalp: lengths may need conditioning while roots need lighter placement or cleansing.
- `less_frizz` vs `curl_definition`: smoothing can relax pattern visually; curl definition often needs leave-in plus hold, not only smoothing oil.
- Buildup removal vs dryness/color protection: clarifying can help dull/coated hair but may worsen dry, damaged, or color-treated lengths if overused.
- Oils/masks vs fine hair: evidence for lubrication/penetration does not mean all oil-heavy routines suit fine or low-density profiles.

## Runtime Implications

- For these goals/concerns, AgentV2 should first name the likely lever category: surface slip/smoothing, damage/porosity, humidity/styling, mechanical handling, buildup/reset, or product weight mismatch.
- Use "moisture" carefully in German runtime copy: map it to Geschmeidigkeit, weniger raues Gefühl, bessere Kämmbarkeit, weniger sprödes Gefühl, and Schutz vor weiterem Austrocknen rather than literal water-loading.
- Default first moves should be low-risk and reversible: conditioner, light leave-in, gentler drying/detangling, amount/placement changes, and occasional reset when buildup signals exist.
- Escalate to rich masks/oils only when profile supports it: thick/coarse/curly/coily/long/damaged/bleached/high-friction lengths or repeated dryness after lighter care.
- For fine hair, volume goal, oily roots, or limp/coated feel, prefer lighter formulas, smaller amounts, mid-length/end placement, and possible reset.
- Runtime guidance should avoid saying any product "repairs" existing cuticle damage back to original state; say it can smooth, coat, reduce friction, improve feel/appearance, and reduce further mechanical damage.
- Ask missing-data questions only when they change the lever: "Fühlt es sich eher trocken/rau an oder eher belegt/schwer?", "Passiert der Frizz vor allem bei Feuchtigkeit?", "Bürstest du Wellen/Locken trocken?", "Sind die Längen gefärbt/blondiert/hitzegestylt?"

## Open Risks

- Much cosmetic hair evidence is lab/tress-based, often ingredient- or sponsor-linked; translate to conservative runtime wording.
- "Porosity" is popular user language but not reliably self-diagnosed; use observable signals instead of treating it as confirmed.
- Oil evidence is not uniform: coconut oil has the clearest penetration/protein-loss evidence, while other oils may mainly lubricate or behave differently by damage state.
- Shine has optical and styling components; do not over-attribute dullness to damage when buildup, lighting, alignment, or product film may explain it.
- Persistent severe breakage, scalp symptoms, sudden hair loss, pain, inflammation, or scaling should leave this cosmetic lane and route to scalp/medical-adjacent guidance.
