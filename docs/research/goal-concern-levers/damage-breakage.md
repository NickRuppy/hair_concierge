## Evidence Labels

| Claim | Evidence | Runtime strength |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Hair fiber is non-living; damaged lengths cannot biologically heal like skin. | `strong` | Say "improve feel/appearance and reduce further damage," not "heal" existing lengths. |
| Conditioner and leave-in/detangler reduce friction, improve combability, and can reduce breakage/split-end risk from handling. | `strong` | First-line cosmetic lever for breakage-prone lengths unless hair is easily weighed down. |
| Reduced heat, lower heat settings, distance/movement while blow-drying, and fewer hot-tool passes reduce damage risk. | `strong` for risk reduction; `moderate` for exact technique hierarchy | Prioritize less/fewer/lower heat before product-only fixes. |
| Bleaching, permanent dye, perming, relaxing/straightening, and repeated chemical services can permanently weaken the shaft and increase breakage/splitting. | `strong` | Chemical history is a major profile modifier and prevention lever. |
| Low-tension handling, loose styles, wide-tooth combs, less tugging, and lower-friction drying reduce breakage risk. | `strong` | Recommend especially for curls/coils, long hair, fragile/bleached hair, and high-shedding users. |
| Split ends can be temporarily smoothed or camouflaged but not permanently repaired once the fiber has split. | `strong` for "no permanent repair"; `moderate` for temporary smoothing | If ends are visibly split/rough, trimming is the only true removal; products are maintenance/camouflage. |
| Bond builders and protein/film-forming treatments can improve strength, feel, or breakage metrics in damaged hair. | `moderate` for some chemistries/in-vitro outcomes; `weak` for broad category claims | Mention as conditional support for chemically/heat-damaged hair, not as guaranteed reversal. |
| Oils/masks alone prevent breakage. | `weak` | Useful for lubrication/feel when matched to hair; not a structural repair default. |
| Hair supplements or nutrition fixes solve ordinary breakage. | `unknown` for breakage without deficiency; medical boundary if shedding/thinning | Do not recommend supplements unless deficiency/medical evaluation is relevant. |

## Practical Lever Map

### `healthier_hair`

Primary levers:

1. `strong` Reduce new damage: lower heat, fewer hot-tool passes, more time between chemical services, avoid overlapping bleach/relaxer/permanent color, protect from high-friction handling.
2. `strong` Use conditioner after shampoo and a leave-in/detangler when hair catches, tangles, or feels rough.
3. `strong` Handle according to texture: straight hair can dry partly before wide-tooth combing; tightly curled/textured hair is usually safer to detangle damp with slip.
4. `moderate` Trim visibly split, thinned, or rough ends when the damage is already structural.

Secondary levers:

- `moderate` Heat protectant before hot tools can reduce measured heat damage/breakage, but does not make high heat harmless.
- `moderate` Bond-building/protein/film-forming treatments may help chemically damaged hair feel stronger and break less; best framed as support, not reversal.
- `weak` Oils/masks can improve lubrication, shine, and feel; use carefully for fine or oily hair.

Cosmetic vs prevention boundary:

- Cosmetic appearance: smoother feel, shine, less frizz, easier combing, less visible roughness.
- Prevention: lower friction, lower tension, fewer chemical/heat insults, trimming split fibers before they continue to snag.
- Not realistic: making old damaged lengths biologically "healthy" again.

### `less_split_ends`

Primary levers:

1. `strong` If ends are already split, trim them; products may smooth the look but cannot permanently fuse the fiber.
2. `strong` Prevent recurrence with less heat, less chemical overlap, conditioner/leave-in, gentle detangling, and less towel friction.
3. `moderate` Keep ends lubricated enough that they do not catch and split further; avoid heavy buildup that forces aggressive cleansing.

Secondary levers:

- `moderate` Dusting/micro-trims can be useful when the user wants to preserve length but split ends are visible.
- `weak` Split-end sealers can temporarily glue/smooth the appearance until washing or wear removes the effect.

When trimming is the only honest answer:

- Ends visibly fork, feather, form white dots/knots, feel rough even after conditioning, or keep tangling at the last few centimeters.
- Hair has severe bleach/heat damage, mushy/stretchy sections, or cortex exposure-like roughness.
- User asks how to "repair" existing split ends permanently.

### `strengthen`

Primary levers:

1. `strong` Define "stronger" as less snapping and better resistance to handling, not faster growth or thicker follicles.
2. `strong` Reduce the main weakening source first: bleach/permanent color/relaxer/perming, high heat, repeated tight styles, rough wet handling.
3. `strong` Improve slip and reduce combing force with conditioner, leave-in, detangler, and wide-tooth/sectioned handling.

Secondary levers:

- `moderate` Bond-building/protein/repair treatments can be reasonable for chemically processed hair, especially if the user already bleaches, colors, relaxes, or heat-styles often.
- `moderate` Heat protectant reduces some heat-styling damage metrics but still belongs below "less heat/fewer passes."
- `weak` "Protein" is not automatically better; too much stiffness or rough feel can worsen handling for some users.

Do not imply:

- That topical strengthening changes the living follicle.
- That all breakage is caused by missing protein.
- That a product can fully reverse severe chemical or thermal damage.

### `anti_breakage`

Primary levers:

1. `strong` Diagnose the pattern: short snapped pieces, rough/frayed ends, uneven lengths, tangling, and flyaways suggest breakage; many full-length hairs from the root suggests shedding/hair loss.
2. `strong` Reduce mechanical stress: detangle with slip, section hair, avoid tugging, lower towel friction, protect hair during sleep if friction is high, avoid tight/continuous tension styles.
3. `strong` Reduce heat/chemical stress and stop overlapping services on fragile lengths.
4. `strong` Condition after shampoo; add leave-in/detangler when breakage happens during brushing/combing.

Secondary levers:

- `moderate` Trim the most damaged ends if breakage starts there; keeping severely split ends can make detangling harsher.
- `moderate` Bond-building/protein/film-forming treatments can support damaged fibers; set expectations as reduced snapping/roughness, not permanent cure.

## Concern Lever Map

### `hair_damage`

First split:

- Cosmetic length damage: rough, dull, frizzy, tangly, porous, split, heat/bleach history.
- Scalp/hair-loss issue: thinning, sudden high shedding, bald patches, pain, inflammation, scaling, or irritation.

Likely levers:

1. `strong` Stop or reduce the active damage source.
2. `strong` Conditioner/leave-in/detangler for friction and combing.
3. `strong` Lower heat and chemical intensity/frequency.
4. `moderate` Trim structurally compromised ends.
5. `moderate` Conditional bond-builder/protein treatment for chemical/heat damage.

Weak first levers:

- More oils/masks without addressing heat, bleach, tight styles, or harsh detangling.
- Supplements for shaft damage without shedding/thinning/deficiency context.

### `split_ends`

First split:

- Existing split ends require removal for a true fix.
- Future split ends can be reduced by prevention.

Likely levers:

1. `strong` Trim/dust visible splits.
2. `strong` Reduce friction and heat/chemical stress.
3. `strong` Condition and detangle gently.
4. `weak` Temporary sealers or smoothing products only for appearance between trims.

Runtime boundary:

- If the user asks "Can I repair split ends?", answer directly: only temporarily smooth; trimming removes them.

### `breakage`

First split:

- Breakage: snapped pieces, uneven short hairs, rough or frayed ends, often after brushing, heat, bleach, tight styles, or tangles.
- Shedding/hair loss: full-length hairs from the scalp/root, diffuse excess hair fall, thinning, bald patches, or stress/illness/postpartum/medication triggers.

Likely levers:

1. `strong` If short snapped pieces dominate: reduce friction/tension, add slip, lower heat/chemical stress, trim worst ends.
2. `strong` If full-length shedding, thinning, patches, scalp symptoms, or sudden major hair fall dominate: shift to medical-adjacent boundary and suggest dermatologist/medical evaluation.
3. `moderate` If both occur, give cosmetic breakage prevention while clearly not treating the shedding cause.

Do not:

- Call shedding "breakage."
- Treat thinning, bald spots, postpartum shedding, medication-related shedding, or sudden diffuse shedding as a conditioner problem.

## Profile Modifiers

- Bleach/permanent color/relaxer/perm: `strong` modifier. Put chemical-stress reduction and salon/professional caution above masks or oils; avoid promising reversal.
- Frequent flat iron/curling iron/hot comb: `strong` modifier. Lower temperature, fewer passes, dry hair before irons, less frequent use, and heat protectant as support.
- Blow-drying: `moderate` modifier. Prefer lower heat, distance, continuous movement, microfiber/towel blotting to shorten heat exposure; avoid close high heat.
- Curls/coils/textured hair: `strong` modifier. Prioritize slip, damp detangling, sectioning, wide-tooth comb/fingers, low-tension styles, and less dry brushing.
- Fine hair: `moderate` modifier. Use lightweight conditioner/leave-in focused on lengths; heavy masks/oils can reduce volume and lead to more cleansing.
- Long hair: `strong` modifier. Ends are older and more weathered; trims, end-focused conditioner, and friction control matter more.
- High-friction routines: `strong` modifier. Towel rubbing, rough brushing, sleeping friction, tight styles, and extensions/weaves can drive breakage even with good products.
- Oily scalp: `moderate` modifier. Keep scalp cleansing separate from length conditioning; avoid using heavy leave-ins at the roots.
- Hair loss/thinning concern: `strong` safety modifier. Separate snapped hair from shed hair and route concerning shedding/thinning to dermatologist guidance.

## Conflicts

- Length retention vs split ends: keeping every centimeter can worsen tangling and breakage when ends are structurally split.
- Volume vs anti-breakage: fine hair may need lightweight slip, not heavy masks/oils that flatten roots.
- Curl definition vs breakage prevention: definition routines should not rely on dry brushing or high-tension manipulation.
- Color goals vs stronger hair: bleach/lightening and repeated permanent color are major damage drivers; prevention may mean slower color changes.
- Sleek styling vs heat reduction: hot tools give cosmetic smoothness but can worsen long-term breakage risk.
- "Repair" language vs evidence: products can improve appearance, slip, measured breakage, or mechanical properties, but severe shaft damage is not fully reversible.

## Runtime Implications

- Always separate three outcomes:
  - Existing appearance: can be smoothed/camouflaged.
  - Future prevention: reduce heat, chemical stress, friction, and tension.
  - Existing structural splits/severe damage: trim is the honest fix.
- For `less_split_ends`, answer trimming directly before product suggestions when the user has visible splits.
- For `breakage`, ask or infer whether hairs are short snapped pieces versus full-length shed hairs. If the answer suggests shedding/thinning, do not continue as routine/product advice only.
- For `strengthen`, define the term in plain language: "less snapping under brushing/styling" rather than "make the hair alive again."
- Put "stop the active damage source" before adding treatments. A bond-builder, mask, or oil should not outrank reducing bleach overlap, high heat, or rough detangling.
- Use conservative German answer shape later:
  - "Das klingt eher nach Haarbruch, wenn es kurze abgebrochene Stuecke sind..."
  - "Spliss kann man optisch glaetten, aber nicht dauerhaft reparieren; wenn die Spitzen schon gespalten sind, hilft nur Schneiden."
  - "Ein Leave-in/Hitzeschutz kann schuetzen, ersetzt aber nicht weniger Hitze oder sanfteres Entwirren."
- Avoid medical claims in cosmetic goals. If the user describes sudden shedding, visible thinning, bald patches, scalp pain, inflammation, or persistent heavy hair fall, hand off to medical-adjacent guidance.

## Open Risks

- Bond-builder evidence is product-chemistry-specific and often in vitro or claim-support oriented; runtime guidance should not generalize every "bond repair" product as proven.
- Heat-protectant data depends heavily on test method, product chemistry, heat level, passes, and whether hair is wet or dry; guidance should stay conditional.
- Split-end severity is hard to know from text. The agent may need a clarifying question or phrase like "wenn die Spitzen wirklich gespalten sind."
- Breakage and shedding can coexist. The runtime must avoid a false either/or when a user reports both short broken pieces and increased root shedding.
- Some professional-practice trim intervals are not strongly standardized; runtime should avoid exact schedules unless user context supports them.
